Vascular Surgery for Plastic Surgeons

How can online coursework be made more effective? How can principles of cognitive and learning science be brought to bear to improve learning outcomes?

We created an online, hour-long module to teach the fundamentals of vascular surgery of the lower extremity to plastic surgery residents. The course was designed to iterate on an existing module used at the University of Pittsburgh Medical Center (UPMC) by applying learning science principles of multimedia and assessment.

We used Cognitive Task Analysis (CTA) with domain experts to analyze and model the decision-making process for diagnosis and treatment of Peripheral Vascular Disease. We worked with partners at the UPMC to specify, categorize, and prioritize the course's learning objectives to suit the needs of UPMC plastic surgery residents.

Based on this data, we re-designed the course to increase interactivity, reduce redundancy, and segment the course content into managable chunks.

We compared our new version of the course against a control version that replicated the original module's content as faithfully as possible in a randomly-assigned experimental study.

Residents took a pretest, followed by a posttest. To the left is a box-and-whisker plot comparing Normalized Gains [(posttest - pretest)/(1-pretest)] across the two conditions. As you can see, the median Normalized Gain was notably higher among students who received the new version of the course (Group B).
